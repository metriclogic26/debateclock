Lincoln-Douglas debate: speech order, prep time & rules

Lincoln-Douglas (LD) is a one-on-one debate format focused on values and philosophy. Named after the 1858 senate debates between Abraham Lincoln and Stephen Douglas, it is one of the three major competitive events governed by the National Speech and Debate Association (NSDA) — alongside Policy debate and Public Forum — and the most widely competed individual debate event in US high schools.

What makes LD distinct: every other major US debate format has at least two-person teams. LD pits one debater against one debater across seven speeches in roughly 40 minutes. Every argument, every cross-examination, every rebuttal — one person, both sides of the issue, prepared and delivered alone. The format rewards philosophical depth over evidence volume, and strategic prioritisation over breadth of argument.

This guide is the complete LD format reference: speech-by-speech timing, prep pool rules, the value-criterion framework that distinguishes LD from other debate formats, how to flow an LD round, how judges evaluate rounds, the traditional-versus-progressive style spectrum, and the major tournaments where LD is competed. Deep-dive resources on case writing, judging, and individual speech strategy are linked throughout — this page is the hub.

Understanding each LD speech

The seven speeches each play a distinct strategic role. Treating them as interchangeable time blocks is the surest way to lose rounds at any level above novice.

Affirmative Constructive (AC)
6:00 · AFFIRMATIVE OPENING

The Affirmative's only opportunity to build the full case before any responses. The AC introduces the value premise, the value criterion, and two to three contentions with supporting evidence. It ends with a brief closing weighing statement that previews how the Affirmative wants the round evaluated. At moderate LD speed (around 300 words per minute), the AC fits roughly 1,800 words. For complete case-writing strategy, see how to write a Lincoln-Douglas case.

Negative Cross-Examination of Affirmative
3:00 · NEGATIVE QUESTIONS

The Negative questions the Affirmative for three minutes. CX is for questioning, not argument — the questioner can ask, demand clarification, or interrupt rambling answers, but cannot make speeches. The answerer cannot ask questions back. CX produces material that gets used in later speeches; what actually happens during CX is not on the flow unless someone references it later. See cross-examination in debate for full CX strategy.

Negative Constructive (NC)
7:00 · LONGEST SPEECH IN THE ROUND

The longest single speech in LD and the most strategically dense. The Negative does two things in 7 minutes: builds their own case (framework, contentions, evidence) and directly responds to the Affirmative case from the AC. The standard split is roughly 4 minutes of NC construction plus 3 minutes of AC refutation, though strong Negatives sometimes weight it differently based on the AC's vulnerabilities. Most strategic choices for the negative are locked in here.

Affirmative Cross-Examination of Negative
3:00 · AFFIRMATIVE QUESTIONS

The Affirmative questions the Negative for three minutes. Same rules as the first CX — Affirmative asks, Negative answers, no speeches, no questions back. The Affirmative usually has more strategic value to extract here because they have the 1AR coming up and need ammunition for the most compressed speech in the round.

First Affirmative Rebuttal (1AR)
4:00 · HARDEST SPEECH IN THE ROUND

Almost universally considered the hardest speech in any US debate format. The 1AR has 4 minutes to respond to everything the Negative just spent 7 minutes establishing — the entire NC (framework, contentions, evidence) plus the Negative's direct attacks on the AC. The compression ratio is brutal: roughly 1 minute of 1AR for every 1:45 of NC. Strong 1ARs are surgical — they identify the highest-leverage Negative arguments, deal with those decisively, and extend the strongest AC contentions with new warrants. Trying to respond line-by-line is usually a losing strategy.

Negative Rebuttal (NR)
6:00 · NEGATIVE'S FINAL SPEECH

The Negative's last opportunity to speak. The NR has to close out the framework debate, decisively win the contentions the Negative is winning, address surviving Affirmative arguments, and do the comparative weighing that tells the judge how to evaluate the round. Strong NRs include explicit overviews ("there are three things the judge has to decide in this round, and here is how each one breaks for me") rather than just walking down the flow.

Second Affirmative Rebuttal (2AR)
3:00 · FINAL SPEECH OF THE ROUND

The Affirmative's final speech and the last words the judge hears. The 2AR is pure rebuttal — by convention, the Affirmative cannot introduce new arguments here because the Negative has no opportunity to respond. The 2AR should crystallise the round: which framework controls, which 1-2 contentions decisively prove the resolution, and explicit weighing of impacts. Three minutes is enough if the speech is planned in advance; 2ARs that try to address everything from the NR usually run out of time before they get to weighing.

How prep time works in LD

Prep time in Lincoln-Douglas is a pool — each debater has 4 minutes total (under NSDA standard) and can use it in any combination before any of their speeches. There is no per-speech limit. A debater might use 3 minutes before the NC and 1 minute before the NR, or all 4 minutes in a single block before the 1AR.

The judge tracks prep time separately for each debater. When a debater calls for prep, the judge starts the clock. When the debater indicates they are ready, prep stops and the remaining time is noted. DebateClock handles this automatically — the prep pool for each side counts down independently and saves state between speeches.

Tournament variations: NSDA standard is 4 minutes. Some Florida tournaments and California regional circuits use 3 minutes. A few national circuit tournaments use 5 minutes. Always check the specific tournament invitation, as prep time changes affect strategy significantly. For broader coverage of how prep pools work across formats, see debate timer prep time explained.

Cross-examination in LD

Cross-examination periods are 3 minutes each and follow the two constructive speeches. The questioner controls the time: they can ask, demand clarification, or interrupt rambling answers. The answerer cannot ask questions back. Neither debater may use prep time during cross-examination.

CX is not a speech — it does not appear on the flow unless someone references it later. What it produces is strategic material: concessions, contradictions, definitions, or evidence challenges that get used in subsequent rebuttals. Strong LD debaters treat CX as setup for arguments they will make later, not as a place to win the round directly.

The two CX periods serve different strategic functions. The first (Negative cross-examining Affirmative) is reconnaissance — clarifying terms in the AC, identifying weak spots in the framework, setting up the NC. The second (Affirmative cross-examining Negative) is preparation for the 1AR — locking in concessions, identifying the strongest Negative argument to prioritise, and surfacing weaknesses in the NC's case construction. For deeper coverage of CX strategy across formats, see cross-examination in debate.

Lincoln-Douglas case structure

Every LD case is built from four standard components: a value premise (the moral concept the case prioritises), a value criterion (the standard for measuring whether the value is upheld), two to three contentions (the substantive arguments), and supporting evidence. The components must connect in a specific way — contentions support the criterion, the criterion measures the value, and the value tells the judge what matters most. Break the chain anywhere and the case collapses.

Affirmative cases run roughly 1,400 to 1,700 words at moderate LD speed. Negative cases are slightly longer (the NC is 7 minutes vs the AC's 6) and have an extra structural choice: substantive negative (own framework plus contentions) versus counter-framework negative (attack the affirmative's framework directly). Strong LD debaters write cases in advance with sharp tags, tight evidence, and explicit weighing built into the structure.

Value and criterion framework

Lincoln-Douglas is a values-based format. Each debater presents a philosophical framework consisting of two components: a value premise (a broad ideal such as justice, liberty, morality, or human dignity) and a value criterion (a standard for measuring whether the value is achieved). The affirmative defends the resolution by arguing their value and criterion better uphold the resolution. The negative argues against — either by accepting the value but contesting the criterion, by proposing a different value entirely, or by arguing that the affirmative's contentions do not actually link to the chosen criterion.

The framework debate happens in the first two speeches (AC and NC), gets developed across the rebuttals, and is usually the decisive layer in any close round. Judges evaluate the round by first determining which framework controls, then assessing which contentions survive under that framework, then weighing surviving impacts. If the framework debate is lost, the substantive contention debate becomes nearly impossible to recover.

Common Lincoln-Douglas frameworks

Most LD cases draw from a relatively small set of philosophical frameworks. Understanding the major frameworks — and which values and criteria pair naturally — is the foundation of competitive LD.

Utilitarianism

The view that the morally right action is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number. Originating with Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill in the 18th and 19th centuries. As a criterion: "maximising utility" or "minimising aggregate harm." Pairs naturally with values like welfare, societal good, or justice (when justice is understood consequentially). Common in resolutions about policy outcomes and competing harms.

Kantian deontology

The view that morality is grounded in duty and the inherent wrongness or rightness of actions, independent of consequences. Drawing from Immanuel Kant's categorical imperative. As a criterion: "respecting the categorical imperative" or "treating persons as ends rather than means." Pairs with values like human dignity, autonomy, or morality. Common in resolutions involving rights, lying, or treatment of vulnerable populations.

Rawlsian justice

The view that just principles are those that would be agreed to behind a "veil of ignorance" — without knowing one's own position in society. Drawing from John Rawls's A Theory of Justice. As a criterion: "the difference principle" (inequalities are justified only if they benefit the least advantaged) or "veil of ignorance." Pairs with values like justice, fairness, or equity. Common in distributive justice resolutions.

Social contract theory

The view that legitimate authority derives from the consent of the governed. Drawing from Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau. As a criterion: "consent of the governed" or "fulfilling the social contract." Pairs with values like governmental legitimacy, liberty, or political obligation. Common in resolutions about state action and individual rights.

Natural rights theory

The view that certain rights exist by nature independent of government recognition. As a criterion: "protecting natural rights" or "non-interference with inherent freedoms." Pairs with values like liberty, individual autonomy, or human rights. Common in resolutions about government overreach.

Capabilities approach

The view, developed by Martha Nussbaum and Amartya Sen, that justice requires ensuring people have the capabilities to live lives they have reason to value. As a criterion: "expanding human capabilities" or "ensuring central capabilities." Pairs with values like human dignity, flourishing, or development. Common in resolutions about welfare state policy and global development.

The right framework for a case depends on what the contentions need, not on which philosophy the debater personally finds most compelling. The case is a strategic tool to win this round, not a statement of personal ethical views. For more on choosing frameworks and pairing them with contentions effectively, see the framework section of how to write a Lincoln-Douglas case.

Flowing in Lincoln-Douglas

Flowing is the process of taking structured notes during a debate round so you can track every argument across every speech. The standard LD flow sheet has 5 columns for the 5 main speeches: AC, NC, 1AR, NR, 2AR. Arguments made in the AC are tracked left to right — if the negative does not respond to an argument in the NC, it is considered "dropped" and the affirmative can extend it in the 1AR as conceded ground.

Flowing is the most important note-taking skill in LD. Dropped arguments decide more LD rounds than any other single factor. Without a flow sheet, judges cannot accurately evaluate what was said vs what was dropped — and debaters cannot prioritise responses across the highly compressed 1AR. Standard notation includes xtnd (extend), resp (response), turn (argument flips against the side that ran it), and drop (unanswered argument, conceded).

Judging Lincoln-Douglas debate

LD judges evaluate three main areas: the framework debate (which value and criterion should govern the round), the substantive arguments (which debater better upholds their framework), and overall presentation and logical consistency. Unlike Policy debate where judges often have years of experience and well-known paradigms, LD draws judges from across the spectrum — parents recruited by their schools, community members, former debaters returning as alumni, coaches, college students, and full-circuit judges.

A judge's decision typically follows this process: first, determine which framework wins — if the affirmative's criterion is accepted, do the affirmative's contentions meet it? If the negative's framework wins, does the negative successfully negate the resolution under that framework? Then evaluate which contentions survive under the controlling framework, then weigh surviving impacts comparatively.

Practical judging notes:

  • Track prep time carefully — each debater has exactly 4 minutes to use freely across the round
  • The 1AR is the most time-pressured speech — 4 minutes to answer a 7-minute NC
  • Dropped arguments in the NR that were clearly extended in the 1AR carry significant weight
  • Speaker points should reflect speaking quality and conduct; the win-loss decision should reflect arguments
  • Always defer to official tournament rules for any timing disputes

Speed and circuit conventions

The same LD format produces dramatically different round styles depending on the tournament circuit. Two broad styles dominate, and the distinction matters because it affects everything from delivery speed to which argument types are acceptable.

Traditional LD

Slower-paced (around 200 words per minute, comparable to a normal political speech), philosophy-focused, value-criterion-centred. Most common at local and regional tournaments, novice and JV divisions, and any tournament with a primarily lay or parent judge pool. Cases are typically delivered conversationally, with emphasis on clear framework debate and persuasive delivery. The dominant style in most parts of the country.

Progressive / circuit LD

Fast (often 350+ words per minute, approaching Policy debate speed), more technical, often includes Policy-style argument types like theory arguments, kritiks, plans, and counterplans. Common at national circuit tournaments — TOC qualifiers, the Tournament of Champions itself, Glenbrooks, the Bronx Round Robin, Harvard. Cases are dense with evidence and require flowing skill to track.

Both styles use identical speech times and round structure. The difference is in delivery speed and argument culture, not format rules. Judges should write a Tabroom paradigm indicating which style they are comfortable with, and debaters should adapt to their judge. A circuit-fast 1AR delivered to a traditional judge who cannot flow at that speed is a guaranteed loss regardless of argument quality.

The LD tournament landscape

Competitive Lincoln-Douglas runs from late August through June, with the heaviest tournament density in February and March (qualifier season) and June (NSDA Nationals).

The structure of competitive LD: most debaters compete primarily at local and state tournaments, with some attending NSDA Nationals or NCFL through district qualification. A smaller subset of debaters pursue the national circuit (TOC qualifiers), which requires extensive travel and runs alongside the local circuit through the year. Both paths produce skilled debaters; they just emphasise different things — traditional LD rewards rhetorical persuasion and philosophical depth, while circuit LD rewards technical efficiency and breadth of argument.

LD resolution structure

Lincoln-Douglas resolutions are value statements, typically in the form "Resolved: [value claim]." Unlike Policy resolutions which propose a specific plan, LD resolutions assert a philosophical position for debaters to affirm or negate. NSDA releases new LD resolutions every two months (September-October, November-December, January-February, March-April, and a separate Nationals topic for May-June), with a separate resolution used at NSDA Nationals.

Resolutions typically address ethical dilemmas, political philosophy, or competing social values. Common resolution structures include:

  • Comparative value judgments: "X is preferable to Y"
  • Obligation statements: "Governments ought to..." or "The United States ought to..."
  • Evaluative claims: "X is morally justified" or "X requires Y"
  • Right-versus-right framings: "When [value 1] conflicts with [value 2], [value 1] ought to prevail"

Each new resolution release prompts a few weeks of community case-writing, evidence-cutting, and topic-specific reading. The bi-monthly cycle means LD debaters become deeply expert in five or six distinct topic areas across a year. Speaker points in LD

Speaker points are scored independently of the win-loss decision and used for seeding (when multiple teams have the same record) and for awards (best speaker awards). The standard NSDA scale runs from roughly 25 to 30, in 0.5 increments at most tournaments and 0.1 at some.

PointsWhat it indicates
30Best speech a judge has ever heard. Reserved for exceptional rounds — once a season or once a career at most.
29 to 29.5Excellent. Clean delivery, strong strategic choices, polished case construction.
28 to 28.5Above average. Solid round at the level of the tournament.
27.5Average. The default starting point for a competent debater.
27Slightly below average — minor issues with clarity, organisation, or strategy.
26Significant issues — unclear delivery, dropped arguments, weak organisation.
25 or belowSerious problems — rudeness, unpreparedness, or technical violations. Used when the judge wants the tournament to take notice.

Speaker point conventions vary by region and tournament. Lay-judge-heavy tournaments often inflate (everyone gets 28+). Circuit tournaments often deflate (27 is genuinely average). Read the tournament invitation or ask the tab room if unsure what range to use. Speaker points are also the appropriate place to reflect speaking quality and conduct issues — a debater who wins on arguments but was rude or unprofessional should still get the win, but with reduced speaker points reflecting the conduct.

Lincoln-Douglas vs other formats

LD is one of three major NSDA formats alongside Policy and Public Forum. The key differences:

  • LD vs Policy: LD is one-on-one with philosophical frameworks. Policy is two-on-two with plan-based advocacy and extensive evidence files. LD round lengths are roughly half of Policy's. Full comparison →
  • LD vs Public Forum: Both are individually accessible formats, but Public Forum uses two-person teams and focuses on current events rather than philosophical values. PF rounds are similar in length to LD but use different speech structure with crossfires instead of one-sided cross-examinations. Full comparison →
  • LD vs World Schools: WSDC uses three-person teams and Points of Information. LD has no POI system and is strictly one-on-one. WSDC is internationally competed; LD is primarily a US format. Full comparison →

Frequently asked questions

What is the LD debate format?
Lincoln-Douglas (LD) debate is a one-on-one competitive debate format. One debater takes the affirmative position and defends a resolution; the other takes the negative position and argues against it. LD uses a value and criterion framework to evaluate philosophical claims. Each debater has 4 minutes of shared prep time to use freely across the round.
What are LD debate speech times?
Affirmative Constructive: 6 minutes. Negative Cross-Examination: 3 minutes. Negative Constructive: 7 minutes. Affirmative Cross-Examination: 3 minutes. First Affirmative Rebuttal (1AR): 4 minutes. Negative Rebuttal: 6 minutes. Second Affirmative Rebuttal (2AR): 3 minutes. Each debater also has 4 minutes of prep time.
What is the 1AR in Lincoln-Douglas debate?
The First Affirmative Rebuttal (1AR) is widely considered the hardest speech in LD. The affirmative has only 4 minutes to respond to the negative's 7-minute constructive. Skilled 1AR debaters prioritize the most important arguments rather than trying to answer everything, and extend their strongest affirmative offense.
How long is a Lincoln-Douglas debate round?
A full LD round lasts approximately 35-40 minutes including prep time usage. The 7 speeches total 32 minutes of speaking time (6+3+7+3+4+6+3). Each debater has up to 4 minutes of prep time, making the maximum round length around 40 minutes.
What is prep time in LD debate?
Prep time is a pool of time each debater uses between speeches to prepare their next speech. In LD, each debater gets 4 minutes of prep time to use freely across the entire round — not per speech. Once used, prep time does not replenish. DebateClock tracks each debater's cumulative prep pool automatically.
What is the LD debate round structure?
An LD round has 7 timed speeches plus prep time. The Affirmative speaks first and last. The Negative has the longest constructive speech at 7 minutes. Cross-examination periods follow the first two constructive speeches. Both debaters have a shared prep pool of 4 minutes each to use between speeches.
How do you judge a Lincoln-Douglas debate?
LD judges evaluate the philosophical framework each debater presents, then assess which side better upheld their value and criterion throughout the round. Judges track prep time usage, speech times, and cross-examination. DebateClock handles timing automatically — select LD from the format picker and all 7 speeches load with correct times and the 4-minute prep pool.
Can you use prep time during cross-examination?
No. Prep time may only be used before a debater's own speeches, not during cross-examination.
What happens if prep time runs out?
The debater must begin their speech immediately when called. Running out of prep time is a competitive disadvantage, not a rule violation.
How is LD different from Policy debate?
LD is one-on-one (Policy is 2v2), has fewer and shorter speeches, and focuses on philosophical values rather than policy advocacy. LD prep pools are 4 min per debater vs 8 min per team in Policy.
What is the difference between traditional and progressive Lincoln-Douglas?
Traditional LD is slower-paced (around 200 words per minute), philosophy-focused, value-criterion-centred, and most common at local and regional tournaments. Progressive (or circuit) LD is faster (often 350+ words per minute), more technical, and often includes Policy-style argument types like theory and kritiks. Both use the same speech times and round structure — the difference is in delivery speed and argument culture, not format rules.
How do speaker points work in Lincoln-Douglas?
Speaker points are scored independently of the win-loss decision and used for seeding and tiebreakers. The standard NSDA scale runs from roughly 25 to 30: 27.5 is average, 28 is good, 28.5 is very good, 29 is excellent, 29.5 is exceptional, 30 is reserved for the best speech a judge has ever heard. Below 26 indicates serious problems. Speaker point conventions vary by region and tournament.
